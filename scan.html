Disparticipation: environmental scan

Matthew Salzano

Prepared for MITH 735

02 December 2019

Introduction

My project considers how digital methods can better understand my nascent theory of disparticipation (Salzano In press) as understood from an image of Angela Peoples at the 2017 Women’s March. To briefly summarize this position, I consider disparticipants those who gather at an assembled protest and exist in a liminal space (between protest/counterprotest and participating/not participating) to make a rhetorical point about the inadequacies of the protest rhetoric. In the case of Peoples, who was pictured holding a sign that said “Don’t forget: White women voted for Trump” in front of three pussyhat-clad white women (Wortham 2017), she was pointing out the racism of white feminism at the 2017 Women’s March. In my paper, I use this representative anecdote to show disparticipation has a rhetorical effect—Peoples’s disparticipation was "documented and circulated, creating a controversy that extends the topoi of activist rhetoric and more robustly includes the issue the disparticipant identified” (Salzano in press). I want to use digital methods to study this rippling effect of disparticipation.

In this environmental scan, I will sketch out how my concern for Peoples’s disparticipation will connect to existing scholarship spanning rhetoric and the digital humanities (DH), specifically Black DH. First, I will chart my stake in the literature about ecological approaches in the humanities, focusing specifically on rhetorical studies—a position I will briefly justify. To this confluence, I specifically want to add why a Black DH approach is important. Next, I want to explore how ecologies and rhetorical studies have been taken up in digital studies. I will explain why I feel data visualization could be a productive method for this digital project. Finally, I will offer provisional conclusions on the status of this literature and suggest how my work might productively map a new node onto this network. Ultimately, I hope to create a DH project that contributes to conversations about rhetorical criticism, digital humanities, and anti-racist social change.

Ecological approaches

In this section, I want to detail my approach to a digital, ecological project. First, I want to offer what conglomeration of authors I will use to stake definitional claims about ecology. Second, I will explain the theoretical and practical reasons why I have opted to focus on rhetorical theories of ecology.

Ecology and the humanities

Several authors in the contemporary humanities have suggested ecology is a useful metaphor for describing postmodern, posthuman life. If postmodernism dispels modernist myths of coherency and suggests the utter fragmentation of culture (McGee 1990) and posthumanism suggests the innate interconnectedness and multiple agencies of humans, technology, objects, the environment (Hayles 1999; Braidotti 2013), then ecology builds off these to provide a way that a critic can offer an understanding of how a text is given meaning. Namely, it emphasizes how texts are given new meaning as they circulate and as one considers the varied agency the text (and its parts) can have. For example, Stephen Muecke (2016) writes that

If these students are taught to look beyond the Author-Text-Reader triangle and to pay attention to the complex ecologies that keep texts alive from place to place . . . they might realize that descriptions of contexts can be fascinatingly extended, and not just in opposition to ‘the text itself.’ Matters of current environmental concern might also have the effect of making the storm itself a more central actor in The Tempest. If so, this is one of the shifting contingencies that sustain the life of the work of art. (246)

The Tempest is given new meaning when read in climate crisis—it reveals that the storm may be an actor, not just a setting. It offers a new reading that, like a new layer on a palimpsest, shows how new meanings are being inscribed on/around/near texts in important ways.

Similarly, Jane Bennett argues for an ecological approach extending vibrancy to matter. Bennett (2010) endorses “a definition of politics as a political ecology and a notion of publics as human-nonhuman collectives that are provoked into existence by a shared experience of harm” (xix, emphasis mine). She believes that extending agency to what has been considered inanimate, instrumentalized matter can help dispel human “earth-destroying fantasies of conquest and consumption” because we are not attuned to the “nonhuman power circulating around and within human bodies” (ix). Bennett and Muecke’s perspectives are important to my project because they emphasize two key contributions of contemporary humanistic studies of ecology: first, that texts are alive and are sustained by varying forces; second, that ecology can be used to understand the power of—and, hopefully, resist—violent colonial logics.

Rhetorical ecology

Jenny Edbauer (now Rice) is quoted throughout the rhetorical literature as the originator of rhetorical ecologies—as of Nov 12, 2019, Google Scholar tells me that her 2005 Rhetoric Society Quarterly article has been cited nearly 450 times. In her germinal piece, she describes how a rhetorical circulation model can supplement a rhetorical situation model. The rhetorical situation (Bitzer 1968) looks at discrete, stable elements: constraints, exigence, rhetor, text, audience. Many authors have challenged this reading as relying on cartesian, liberal, modernist assumptions and thus inadequate for describing rhetoric—persuasion necessarily exceeds the mind/body, discrete situational elements approach (Biesecker 1989; Chaput 2010; Vatz 1973). Edbauer (2005) suggests:

[The rhetorical s]ituation bleeds into the concatenation of public interaction. Public interactions bleed into wider social processes. The elements of rhetorical situation simply bleed. . . . Rather than primarily speaking of rhetoric through the terministic lens of conglomerated elements, I look towards a framework of affective ecologies that recontextualizes rhetorics in their temporal, historical, and lived fluxes. (9)

She uses this model to trace the phrase "Keep Austin Weird" through its varied contexts—not simply tracing its history but tracing how its meaning fundamentally changed as it took on new affective association in its transit. Shepley (2013) describes this tracing as understanding the relations: the text as a node that is connected to and connects many other nodes (see also Pfister 2014).

I have chosen to attune to rhetorical ecology for a few reasons. The most straightforward: I am a Rhetoric Ph.D. student and it is professionally advantageous for me to situate myself in this literature and publish findings in my field. But there is a conceptual reason to connect the digital humanities and rhetoric here: there are some DH studies about ecology (Poole 2017; Waters 2013) but they tend to use the term ecology loosely rather than as a central metaphor to guide an understanding of a text. What rhetorical ecology can provide is a way to connect longstanding and presently emphasized DH commitments to social change (Gold and Klein 2019) and DH knowledge about networks (Moretti 2011). The data collecting and visualizing tools of the digital humanities can connect with a rhetorical ecology focus on being persuasive. Rivers and Webers (2011) suggest that, in the classroom, an ecological approach helps students create nuanced persuasive material.

"[W]e encourage students to navigate and negotiate worldly encumbrances and the conventions of their publics by focusing on rhetoric as the way that immanent, affective, timely, and contingent truths are invented. Not only must advocacy adapt to ecological constraints, but advocacy goals should necessarily arise from within rather than outside an ecology. (190)

This, to me, is one method that could lead to additions to Gold and Kelin’s (2019) list of projects that “can contribute in concrete and meaningful ways to improving the situation of those most affected by the toxic turn brought about by the 2016 presidential election and its aftermath” (para. 4). Rhetorical ecology has the tools to find what might be ‘concrete and meaningful,’ and Bennett and Muecke help show how ‘toxicity’ can be revealed in ecological analyses. But in the next section, I want to suggest that ecological studies of digital rhetoric need to be supplemented with black DH if they wish to truly address the ‘toxic turn’ showcased in the 2016 election.

Digital rhetorical ecology and black DH

Ecology has been used in rhetorical studies to theorize and critique a variety of things, digital and non-digital (Rickert 2013; Boyle 2016; Stormer and McGreavy 2017). Most recently, in Post-digital rhetoric and the new aesthetic, Justin Hodgson (2019) uses a theory of rhetorical ecology to understand an aesthetic movement. The New Aesthetic, first recognized in a Tumblr blog assembled by James Bridle, implicate how a variety of aesthetic objects that reveal assemblages and slippages among presumed binaries of human and nonhuman, digital and real. For example, Aram Bartholl’s MAP, ‘Hello World,’ created a massive Google Maps bubble, usually relegated to the screen, as a physical object (Hodgson 2019, 77–78).

The posts on The New Aesthetic Tumblr span many objects and resist easy definition. But with an ecological approach, Hodgson (2019) is not worried about explaining why this aesthetic is still important for study:

[R]hetorical power comes not from the imposition (or fit) of a particular categorical order (nor its related cause-effect logics) but rather from describing the fibers and fissures that formulate the flux: the relations, flows, and points of contact among the entities and organisms of a rhetorical ecology, and the social, institutional, and historical backdrops against which artifacts and utterances move. (39)

Understanding that this complex system of relations is what makes meaning—a rhetorical ecology—he opts to describe the new aesthetic in four unstable contours that are crafted by varied arguments about and from The New Aesthetic as well as objects in Bridle’s collection.

Hodgson’s study is fascinating and useful to consider how to write criticism about elusive, digital movements that do not assemble in as recognizable a form as, say, a protest on the national mall. It makes Hodgson’s study incredibly applicable to disparticipation, a position only revealed when one considers the broad sense of rhetorical relationships that disguise a disparticipant (by attempting to sort them as participating/not participating; protesting/counterprotesting) but also give that disparticipant a unique position to be persuasive outside of dominant logics that lead to toxicity. That critical political view is made possible with rhetorical ecology, as Shepley (2013) argues:

Rather than look at one individual’s choice to speak or write, her study of changes in a message’s rhetorical work prompts us to consider: who made each of those changes? Who benefited from each change? Thus, room exists in which to see rhetorical ecologies as a more social, dynamic version of . . . rhetorical activity. (para. 11)

But Hodgson’s (2019) book does not give us any sense of how to do this post-digital analysis with an eye for power, for asking the “who” questions necessary for critical political analysis. Hodgson does provide the jumping-off point, considering the relationship of human-technology assemblages to relationship practices (75) but he does not turn to critical discussions of how this rhetorical theorizing implicates race.

This allows me to supplement Hodgson's ecology with the questions and practices of Black DH. Moya Bailey’s (2015) concept of digital alchemy, for example, shows how “Black women . . . transform everyday digital media into valuable social justice media magic that recodes failed dominant scripts” (para. 5). Her study of redefining representations shows how Black trans women curated media to resist problematic representations (para. 3). Recast in the language of rhetorical ecology, the meaning of these tweets were productively changed by Black trans women for an anti-racist, an anti-transmisogynoir agenda. One might ask about Angela Peoples: how did her image change conversations, and how did her image change in those conversations? What nodes can be networked to see how new meaning was produced in the ecological circulation of this image? Bailey and others importantly ask how this meaning is made within Black online communities (Steele 2015; Florini 2014; Hill 2018; Parham 2019). But Peoples’s claim, made about and in an assembly of White women, merits furthering these inquiries by studying how this image circulated in a variety of racial contexts to make meaning about race: Peoples’s race, the race of the marchers, and the racism of the march.

To do this, we must ask—in a posthumanist spirit—“How is Angela People’s relationship to the concept of human modified in the circulation of this image?” Armond Towns (2018) has provocatively challenged rhetoricians to consider how the presumption of human has undergirded racism, specifically racist rhetorical inquiry. In his essay, he argues that “What this means is that the Black female slave was not much different from matter or media: Violence against her was a tool, a vehicle through which man continued to maintain his dominance over nature” (354). Hodgson suggests (post-digital) rhetoricians attune to human-technology assemblages as participants in creating rhetoric but does not critically evaluate who gets to be included as human and who gets to be included as technology. Does Peoples serve as technology—as media (cf. Towns 2018)—for white people to advance claims about transformative politics, or does she recover her humanity (cf. Gallon 2016)? In the next section, I will suggest that collecting tweets about Peoples’s image and visualizing this data may provide one way to start answering this question.

Digital rhetoric and data visualization

This section is unfortunately short: there is a dearth of digital rhetoric projects in the vein I envision. There are digital book-like works, like Jason Helms’s (2017) Rhizcomics and his study of rhetorical games (Helms 2019). There are scholarly books about making video criticism, like The Videographic Essay (Keathley, Mittell, and Grant 2019). These projects are interesting, but most do not use digital methods to understand something differently: they use digital methods to present work in innovative and exciting ways. My impression of the scholarship is that rhetoricians see themselves primarily as contributing to DH as a mode of presentation (either rhetoricians helping DH be more persuasive, or DH helping rhetoricians display their work persuasively). For example, one digital rhetoric project has been done on Peoples already. Laura Tetreault’s (2019) webtext is a scholarly article written for the web: the most digital it gets, in terms of taking advantage of the form, is the embedding of a now-broken Twitter feed that is supposed to display live Tweets about Peoples and the Women’s March.

I would extend what Ridolfo (2015) has noted—that “more work remains to articulate projects in rhetorical terms to broader DH audiences” (n.p.)—and add that very few DH projects have taken off in rhetorical studies as digital-rhetorical inquiry. Related fields have done complementary work. For example, there is social media communication research (Jackson and Welles 2015; Jackson, Bailey, and Foucault Welles 2018) that uses network maps and makes formative claims about counterpublics to do interesting digital inquiry. Their studies serve as models of collecting Twitter data and mapping it. But the maps are not manipulatable to the reader of the text and are not used in service of rhetorical claims about the circulation of a text, rather they are qualitative studies about community-building—they aren’t rhetorical inquiry. New DH rhetoric work on the 2017 Women’s March is coming, however, as my colleague (and former AADHum scholar) Alyson Farzad-Phillips is in revision process with a project on the march, mapping, and gentrification for Women’s Studies in Communication.

Ultimately, I imagine something more like Yale’s Syllagraphics. The project, which has seemingly gone nowhere since 2013, was going to feature an “assigned reading network” map that connected women’s studies courses (e.g., WGSS 296 and 372) via their shared readings (e.g., both read Gayle Rubin). These nodes were marked so that a user could use the map to see the situation differently. The courses are no longer separate, but rather living in an ecology where students shared experiences with authors across courses interact and visually connect different and disparate courses, suggesting new ways to make meaning in the curriculum. Other, similar projects include David McClure’s "Humanist List visualization". McClure’s dataset of 12 million words written over 27 years of a humanist listserv is visualized as a network that takes into account year of post, frequency of word appearance, and its relationship/similarity to other words. His project Open Syllabus uses a similar style to display related items of a syllabus.

I would hope to display my network in an interactive form like Syllagraphics and McClure’s work, but I would focus on a much smaller dataset and phenomenon in order to make some critical insight. An example of this might be Kaima L. Glover and Alex Gil’s “In The Same Boats”. They focus on just a few key humanist researchers that interacted with each other throughout the 20th century—in the process, they reveal how rhetorical the supposed separation of the intellectual work of the ‘Global South’ and the ‘West’ is. I imagine a network that links different nodes (tweets, maybe also blog posts and Reddit posts) that allows for this same sort of meaning-making potential: revealing new insights to me, as a critic who can think about an ecology differently as it is visualized, and to others who may view the network.

Conclusions: hopes for building a richer ecology

To briefly summarize this environmental scan: first, I explained ecological approaches to literature and rhetoric and explained why rhetorical ecology and digital humanities are a complementary pairing. Second, I read post-digital rhetorical ecology against the grain of Black DH and black new materialist studies to reveal how this project will contribute to the literature on ecology. Third, I showed how this work would further the work at the intersection of rhetoric and digital humanities. I hope that this project can shed new light on the image of Peoples and, in grappling with the confluence of forces acting on the project, model how rhetorical studies and digital humanities can be invested in an anti-racist foundation at its very roots of analysis and methodology.
