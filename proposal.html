Seeking resonances of ‘Disparticipation'

1. Topic

My theory of “Disparticipation” and an image of Angela Peoples at the 2017 Women’s March.

I started working on a project called “Disparticipation” in its current form in Spring 2019 (the idea first started in Fall 2016). I suggest that social movement studies in rhetoric do not adequately account for people who neither protest nor counter-protest, participate nor not participate. I call this in-between space disparticipation. “Building off of Jose Esteban Muñoz’s (1999) theory in Disidentifications, I contend that disparticipation is the action of someone who takes part in an assembly queerly by defying global understandings of social movement” (Salzano, forthcoming). This paper was presented and is (barring unforeseen circumstances) going to be published in the proceedings of the 2019 Alta Argumentation Conference. In it, I suggest that:

I will use the image of Angela Peoples at the 2017 Women’s March as a representative anecdote that shows how disparticipation ripples through activist discourse in three steps. (1) A disparticipant recognizes a structural issue inadequately addressed in the protest rhetoric surrounding them. This leads them to (2) participate in a movement queerly, that is, by not clearly participating or not participating, protesting or counterprotesting. This queer participation is (3) documented and circulated, creating a controversy that extends the topoi of activist rhetoric and more robustly includes the issue the disparticipant identified. (Salzano, forthcoming).

In this DH project, I want to see if I can visualize how Angela Peoples’s disparticipation spreads on digital platforms. At this nascent stage of development, I am curious if I can use an application like Gephi paired with data extracted from twitter and gathered from the blogosphere to reveal how this image spread. Specifically, I am curious if it reveals something about theories of rhetorical ecologies and digital circulation, something currently of interest in rhetoric, composition, and communication. This project would lead to either a digital supplement to my existing paper (which will be expanded and submitted to a Communication journal this summer) or as a new paper (that would be conferenced next year).

2. Research question

Most broadly, my question is: how do we account for people who gather at protests but cannot easily be classified as protestors or counterprotestors? Since my rhetorical research so far suggests a concept of disparticipation, I’m curious at if it is possible to trace that disparticipation.

Thus, for this project, my questions are:

  1. Did the image of Angela Peoples at the 2017 women’s march help extend black feminist criticism of the women’s march? To what publics?
  2. How did the meaning of the image and Peoples’s image change during/because of its circulation?

    At a broader disciplinary scale, my contribution is by using this gathered data to answer the question:

  3. Can rhetorical ecologies be usefully visualized?

3. Areas/subjects

This study will draw upon and contribute to a few key research areas.

  1. Rhetorical ecologies (especially: Edbauer 2005; Bennett 2010; Chaput 2010; Walsh and Boyle 2017; Hodgson 2019).
  2. Rhetorical studies of social movement (namely: McGee 1980; Foust et al 2017; Foust and Hoyt 2018)
  3. Black DH (Brock 2010; Steele 2018; Lu and Steele 2019)
  4. Rhetoric and digital humanities (e.g. Ridolfo and Hart-Davidson, 2015)

I feel relatively confident that I will know how to frame my research question within the first two areas of study, but I am in need of support and tutelage for understanding how my work can be enriched by work in (3) Black studies + DH, especially Black Feminism + DH, and (4) Rhetoric + DH.

Rhetorical Ecology

In his book, Post-Digital Rhetoric and The New Aesthetic, Hodgson uses rhetorical ecology as his method to analyze the aesthetic movement The New Aesthetic. The movement, lacking a formal manifesto or principles, is only visible in its “shadows and silhouettes of momentary figurations” (Hodgson 2019, 37). He clarifies Edbauer’s (2005) foundational piece in rhetorical ecology:

“In this reorientation, the rhetorical power comes not from the imposition (or fit) of a particular categorical order (nor its related cause–effect logics) but rather from describing the fibers and fissures that formulate the flux: the relations, flows, and points of contact among the entities and organisms of a rhetorical ecology, and the social, institutional, and historical backdrops against which artifacts and utterances move. Rhetoric (and/or the rhetorical significance of any artifact or utterance) becomes constituted, then, not through declamation by a critical hermeneutist but rather as emergent among/through networked ‘flows and connections’” (Hodgson 2019, 39).

I cite this at length not to fill the page, but because I am still struggling with what this means. For Edbauer and Hodgson, it means they select examples that show how similar discursive fragments (like the sticker “Keep Austin Weird,” for Edbauer) re-appear and move around different publics and situations and reveal the edges (in the networked sense) and boundaries of different communities and/or phenomena. This seems like prime data to be visualized, if one can show how some image or phrase — in my case, the photo of Peoples — moves and is used differently in its various substantiations.
